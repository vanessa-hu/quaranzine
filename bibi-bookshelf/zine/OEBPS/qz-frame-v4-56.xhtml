Section I: Chinatowns and Xenopho-bia During the Coronavirus Pandemic

While Chinese food has become widely popularized in today’s society, the general public’s devastating aversion to Chinese food and restaurants today in response to the outbreak of COVID-19 demonstrates how prevalent anti-Asian xenophobia still remains deeply embed-ded in the social, cultural, and political fabric of society. This simultaneous cele-bration for the economic success of eth-nically exotic Chinatowns while being vilified as filthy and diseased perpetuates the notion that we, as Asian Americans, are still foreign and racialized bodies out of place in Westernized cultures...

Section II: Historical Contextualiza-tion of Xenophobia against Chinese Americans and Chinese Food

Similar efforts were taken to

scapegoat the Chinese for the bubonic plague, attacking Chinatown as dirty, non-native (smelly), and connecting Chinese food to rats. In the 1900s, California was hit by the bubonic plague, a devastating epidemic spread from Asian trade routes to European countries and then the Amer-icas. Not only was it deadly, assaulting the lymphatic system and causing “black boils that oozed blood and pus,”1 but it was also highly infectious. 1 After the US public health service announced that this Black Death was spread through rats and fleas, people immediately found reason to blame the Chinaman. As previously mentioned, anti Chinese sentiment had already been established. In addition, San Francisco’s Chinatown was dilapidated to say the least. Property law at the time did not allow Asians to own land so they had to rely on well-networked white men who directed them to owners of tenement houses who preferred Chinese residents because they reliably paid their rent but themselves ne-glected their properties.2 The Chinese en-dured such conditions, deciding not to pay for any fixtures in order to 2 send income back home. Chinatown’s unsanitary state was thus not a product of any uncleanli-ness of the Chinese but of neglect by prop-erty owners.
