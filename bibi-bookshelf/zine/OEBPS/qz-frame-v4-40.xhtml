IV

I can’t remember a time when I didn’t know about the incarceration of Americans of Japanese Ancestry (AJAs) during World War II, but I didn’t get into the details until high school. They showed us cartoons depicting every Japanese person and AJA identically with buckteeth, a thin moustache, and downward curving lines for eyes behind circular glasses. They showed us diagrams differentiating AJAs from Chinese Americans: we’re yellower, shorter, wider, hairier, and have flatter noses, shorter faces, and single eyelids above slanty eyes. Even the “positive” stories were unpleasant: Fred Korematsu, our sup-posed hero, got double eyelid surgery to hide his ancestry before trying and famously failing to challenge his incarceration in court.

I had known about the injustices of the concentration camps, questionnaires, slurs, and property losses, but it had never previously occurred to me that the incarcer-ation would have involved cruelty about appearances, too. I wondered if white people who looked at me only saw my slanted eyes, “yellow” skin, and weird teeth and find them repulsive. I thought more about my double eyelids and relatively big eyes, too. Did they make me less Japanese? Did I want that? Did white people think my eyes were large? I decided it was wrong to see double eyelids as better than singles and finally realized that most white people would see my face and see me as inferior no matter what job I got, what clothes I wore, or what foods I refused to eat.

V

In sophomore year of college, after years of failed conversion attempts from my sister, I plummeted down the kpop rabbit hole after watching Twice’s legendary What is Love music video. As I waded through the endless stream of content, I quickly noticed, among many other things, that, contrary to popular (white) opinion, kpop idols definitely don’t all look the same. Even individual idols looked radically different from show to show, as groups reinvent their appearances with each new release: new hair, new outfits, new attitudes. In spite of the industry’s imposing beauty standards laced with fatphobia and colorism and bad habit of treating whiteness as an ideal and blackness as a costume, in these idols I saw a million ways to be undeniably Asian and undeniably beautiful. They all looked like supermodels, but some of them also kind of looked like my cousins. Some idols had narrower or slanted eyes, single eyelids, wider jaws, flatter noses, or bigger teeth, and they were all gorgeous and revered for their beauty, full stop – not seen as just exoti-cally sexual or cute in an Asian way or any of that bullshit.
