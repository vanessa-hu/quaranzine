12

It was a breathless summer: no high-rise,

no glowing street, no delicate ache

of a long trip chewing on your ankles—

you no longer go toward it. Yet it still

sits heavily within you, a summer existing

in liminal spaces, on the precipice

between ages. Change lifted by water,

humidity curling your growing hair,

which only a year ago you had cut

above your shoulders. That July

was before you knew anything;

you stood with your grandmother

at the fruit stand and bought

dark red cherries, stained your fingers on them

and taught your curiosity to dance.

Now your grandmother jumps an ocean length

through video call, flickers a bit

before she smiles. You’ve forgotten her hands

already; the smell of her cooking is next to go.

“Will you be alone by the fall?” she asks,

mouth forming a worried line that mirrors her brow.

“Will you be lonely?”

The summer rumbles loudly in your ears,

and no answer forms. You have not felt like a child

for a long time, but recently you do.

It has something to do with the helplessness

of your grandmother’s weak Internet signal, the way

her image freezes into an unexpected portrait,

but the audio does not cut, so you can still hear her

calling for you.

This time, you try to call back, but it’s too late.

A mumble of static,

and she disappears.

Time Lapse

by Grace Wang
