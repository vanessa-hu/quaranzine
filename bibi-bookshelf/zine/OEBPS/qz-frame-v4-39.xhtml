I knew this was stupid: they say about a million times that Harry has his mother’s green eyes. Had they even read the books? His eye color was very important. I wondered a little how J.K. Rowling would describe me – I had my mother’s eyes too, but they were the same shade of brown as the eyes of almost everyone I knew.

Not much later, I discovered Percy Jackson. Percy felt closer to home than Harry, not just because he lived in the US but because his voice sounded like mine: sassy, unsure, mischievous. But only a few chapters in, it became clear that I wasn’t like Percy because demigods got their godly parents’ magical eyes. They had vibrant colors – cloudy grey, seafoam green – and would fill with lighting or storms in battle. I had thought my eyes were special because of their shape, their size, and the wrinkles around them, but I was increasingly sure they were boring. I started to want the stormy grey irises of a child of Athena and wonder why my eyes wouldn’t flash with anger or cloud with sadness, what-ever that even looked like. I grew increasingly worried as I read his descriptions that Percy wouldn’t be able to tell me apart from my friends.

III

In sixth grade, I met the coolest boy. He was a little tanner than I was from skateboarding and the beach. He was quiet in class. He smelled a little like sesame oil and his arms and legs were always covered in scratches. He had long hair for a boy, black and thick and straight, with bangs that sometimes covered his dark brown eyes. His eyes were narrow, with thin, pronounced bags under them. He had single eyelids and lashes that grew down-ward. When he smiled, which was rare, his eyes narrowed until you couldn’t really see them. That year, I wormed my way closer to him, giving sage advice about his girlfriend and helping him with homework. We gmail messaged almost every night for hours. I thought about him when I listened to Sara Bareilles. When high school began, we had a big fight and stopped talking.

In tenth grade, I was in a quartet with a new cellist. We waited for our parents together at the music building after school most days. His posture was phenomenal, al-most infuriatingly so, even though he was always sleepy. He smiled with his acne-covered cheeks. His upper eyelids were smooth, and the corners of his eyes came to sharp points. He had large dark brown irises. He didn’t talk much but made a lot of casual physical contact. We texted incessantly for a few months, mostly about the girl he liked. After that, his replies grew infrequent and slow, and I grew sadder. I began to wonder if I had like liked him and maybe my gmail friend, too. But when I thought about their faces, I could see their imperfections in detail, and on some level, I counted their eyes among those im-perfections. I told myself that if I thought about them this way, I just saw them as friends, which made perfect sense, of course, because I wasn’t gay.
